## Key Citations for Follow-up

### Synthesis-Focused

1. **Fant, G. (1960). Acoustic theory of speech production.** — Foundational theory of how acoustic properties relate to vocal tract configuration. Essential for understanding how formant patterns encode articulation.

2. **Liberman et al. (1952). The role of selected stimulus variables in the perception of unvoiced stop consonants.** — Classic synthetic speech experiments demonstrating that release bursts and formant transitions are both sufficient cues for stop recognition. Directly relevant to PLSTEP burst implementation.

3. **Fischer-Jørgensen, E. (1954). Acoustic analysis of stop consonants.** — Detailed acoustic analysis of stop consonant properties including burst characteristics across places of articulation.

### Place of Articulation Cues

4. **Cole & Scott (1974a, 1974b). The phantom in the phoneme & Toward a theory of speech perception.** — Demonstrates that burst alone can signal stop place and discusses the equivalence of bursts and transitions as cues, with consideration of context-dependency.

5. **Delattre, Liberman, & Cooper (1955). Acoustic loci and transitional cues for consonants.** — Seminal work on how formant transitions encode place of articulation across vowel contexts.

## Paper Summary

**Dorman, Studdert-Kennedy, & Raphael (1977)** conducted three experiments investigating whether release bursts and formant transitions are functionally equivalent as acoustic cues to stop-consonant place of articulation. Using natural speech stimuli modified to isolate or combine burst and transition components, they found that bursts carry significant perceptual weight, particularly for labial and apical stops before rounded vowels. The study emphasizes that while acoustic variations in burst effectiveness occur across vowel contexts, these variations follow from acoustic properties of the stops themselves (spectral content, front-cavity resonance) rather than from true functional inadequacy of the bursts as cues. The results support an account of speech perception based on tracking vocal-tract resonances rather than relying on single invariant acoustic features.
